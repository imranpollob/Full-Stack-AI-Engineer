# Week 4: Probability and Statistics for Machine Learning

Welcome to Week 4 of the Full-Stack AI Engineer bootcamp! We spent last week learning the core mechanics of machine learning mathematically: predicting and optimizing using Algebra and Calculus. 

This week, we take a step back and look at the data itself.

AI models are data hungry. But not all data is created equal. Before you ever hit `.fit()` on a neural network, you must understand the shape, reliability, and correlations hidden inside your dataset. This week is entirely dedicated to **Statistical Analysis**.

## What We'll Cover This Week

*   **Day 1: Probability Theory and Random Variables.** We will learn the foundation of probability—calculating likelihoods, understanding independence, and mapping events to Random Variables.
*   **Day 2: Probability Distributions.** Random variables aren't purely random; they follow patterns. We will explore the mathematical curves that dictate data: Gaussian, Binomial, Poisson, and Uniform distributions.
*   **Day 3: Statistical Inference & Confidence Intervals.** How much of a dataset do you need to look at to know truth? We learn how to take small samples of data and estimate the entire population's parameters using Confidence Intervals.
*   **Day 4: Hypothesis Testing & P-Values.** You believe your AI model is better than the baseline. But can you prove it mathematically? We introduce the Null Hypothesis and the ever-important P-Value.
*   **Day 5: Types of Hypothesis Tests.** We expand on Day 4, covering specific tests for specific data types: T-Tests (for means), Chi-Square (for categories), and ANOVA (for multiple groups).
*   **Day 6: Correlation and Regression Analysis.** Before feeding 100 features into a neural network, you should know which ones actually matter. We learn how to mathematically score the relationship between columns of data.
*   **Day 7: The Final Project - Real-World Statistics.** We wrap up the week by taking a real, messy dataset and performing a complete, rigorous Statistical Analysis pipeline from top to bottom.

## Why This Matters for AI
You can build the most advanced Neural Network on the planet, but if you train it on statistically biased data, or evaluate its success without accounting for variance, it will fail in production. 

Machine learning *is* applied statistics. This week gives you the theoretical armor you need to defend the models you build.
